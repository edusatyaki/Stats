Chapter One
Basic
Probability
Quantifying uncertainty. From coin flips to expected values — the mathematics of chance.
Chance Events & Sample Spaces
The "Sample Space" is simply the list of all possible things that can happen. For a coin, it's just {Heads, Tails}.
Expected Value
If you repeat an experiment forever, what is the average outcome? It's the "long-run" mathematical expectation.
Variance & Spread
How wild are the swings? Variance measures how far outcomes typically stray from the Expected Value.

Story 1: The Gambler’s Fallacy
Theory
P(H) = P(T) = 0.5

Imagine a gambler named 'Lucky Louie'. He flips a coin 5 times, and it lands Heads every single time. Louie is convinced that Tails is "due." He bets his entire stack on Tails, believing the next outcome must correct the streak.

This is the Gambler's Fallacy. Watch the data stabilize over many flips. Does Louie have a valid strategy?

Chapter Three
Probability
Distributions
From individual outcomes to the shape of randomness. The famous bell curve and its siblings.
Random Variables
A variable whose value depends on a random event, like the sum of two rolled dice.
Discrete vs. Continuous
Discrete: things you can count (shoes). Continuous: things you measure (temperature, height).
Central Limit Theorem
The magic rule: If you add up enough random things, their average will always form a Normal "Bell" curve!

Normal Distribution
Story 3: QC on the Production Line
Parameters
N(μ, σ²)
μ (mean) shifts the center. σ (std dev) controls the spread. A low σ means data is tightly clustered.
The Story: Food Packing

A factory is trying to optimize the weight of cereal boxes. The ideal weight is 30oz (μ = 30). The quality control team sets μ to the target.

If the packing machine is erratic, the standard deviation is high (σ = 2). This models error or 'slop'. Try changing σ to model the effects of machine error on production consistency.

Chapter Four
Frequentist
Inference
Drawing conclusions from a sample. Estimating unknowns and testing hypotheses with confidence.
Point Estimation
Using a sample statistic (like a sample average) to guess the true population parameter.
Confidence Intervals
A range of values we are fairly sure contains the true unknown value. "Margin of Error."
Hypothesis Testing
Calculating p-values to determine if an effect is real, or just statistical noise/luck.

Story 4: Cafe Spend Estimation
95% CI
x̄ ± 1.96 · (σ/√n)
A confidence interval is like throwing a net to catch a fish (the true mean, μ). A 95% CI means if we throw 100 nets, 95 will catch the fish.
The Story: Cafe Spend

A coffee shop owner at 'Café Caffeine' wants to know the true average customer spending to plan a new menu. They can't survey everyone, so they sample 10 customers.

This single sample (1 Net) is just an estimate. However, taking multiple samples (100 Nets) is testing their sampling *method’s* confidence.

Chapter Five
Bayesian
Inference
Updating beliefs with evidence. Bayes' theorem is a mathematical machine for rational learning.
Bayes' Theorem
A formula that describes how to update the probabilities of hypotheses when given evidence.
Prior Beliefs
What you believe *before* you see the data. A "flat" prior means you think all outcomes are equally likely.
Posterior Updates
Your updated belief *after* factoring in the new data. The Prior + Data = Posterior.

Chapter 05 · Bayesian Inference
Prior → Data → Posterior
Story 5: Catching a Casino Cheat
Bayes' Rule
P(A|B) ∝ P(B|A)·P(A)
The Posterior is proportional to the Likelihood of the data times your Prior belief.
The Story: Rigged Coin

A casino manager suspects a coin is rigged to favor Heads (bias θ). They start testing it.

They set a 'True bias' (p) as a hidden parameter. Each flip (gathering data) updates the 'MAP estimate' (best guess) from a 'Prior Belief' to a refined 'Posterior Belief' (purple curve), visually converging on the hidden truth.

Chapter Six
Regression
Analysis
Modeling relationships. Finding the line of best fit that explains how one variable predicts another.
Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)
The algorithm that draws the line. It works by minimizing the squared vertical distances from the dots to the line.
Correlation (r)
Measures how tightly the dots cluster around the line. +1 is a perfect positive relationship; 0 is random noise.
R-Squared (R²)
The percentage of the outcome that can be explained by your predictor. (e.g. 80% of test scores are explained by hours studied).
